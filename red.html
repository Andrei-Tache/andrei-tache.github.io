Red is the result of a 2-month design challenge I set to myself so I could improve my level design skills.
I kept the mechanical design to a minimum so I could mostly focus on blocking out the level and creating fun challenges.

I also took this oportunity to experiment with some other elements, like using cutscenes to set the game's story and the off-beat art style.
[side view map]

I started by coming up with a number of challenge sections on paper and deciding how to connect them together in terms of difficulty and mechanics used.

I keep to a simple linear path through the level to make the design process simpler and prioritise the quality and polish of each section instead of trying to connect them together in a more complex way.
[top-down map]

After deciding on the order of the sections, I drew a rough map to see where the zones would connect to each other. This served as a good reference when building the level in-game, as I knew in which direction the level should head towards.

After this prototyping stage, I found that the 2d nature of paper design would limit the way I would create levels, making them feel very flat and uninteresting to traverse.
Switching to designing them in the editor directly helped me use the full 3d space much better.
[stairs zone drawing and in-game screenshot]

For each section, I would roughly block out the playable area inside of Unity using ProBuilder, which let me quickly make changes to level geometry without having to export and re-import assets from another program, speeding up the iteration process considerably.

I would then walk through the area and make slight adjustments until it began to fit with the theme of each section. I would then introduce the challenge elements and play through the zone, modifying their placement where needed, until passing through the section felt fluid and satisfying.

At the end, I would add in a little bit of visual flair to differentiate each area and mark the progress the player makes through the full level.
[coridors with cables (progress video?)]





The biggest mistake I made with this challenge is that I didn't allocate more time to playtesting, instead using all of the time to create the game and releasing it with close to no testing from other people other than me.

If I did this challenge again today, I would make playtesting the central point so I could compare the effects of each change and get a deeper intuition for how a good level is layed out.
