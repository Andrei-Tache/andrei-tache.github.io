Andrei Tache
Systems Designer
Games About Me Itch.io
My goal when making this game has been to introduce people to cool musical instruments and teach them how to play songs on them.

As someone with no musical background, the ability to turn ‘random’ pitches played at a specific tempo into a beautiful melody feels like sorcery to me, and discovering it by playing the written notes feels like uncovering a hidden treasure.

I wanted my game to convey the same sense of wonder and curiosity to my players.
The key pilars I based my design decisions on were:

1. Exploration and play: Encourage players to try out the instruments they find most interesting and figure out how they work with minimal hand-holding.

2. Learn to play instruments: Have the instruments play as close to their real-life counterparts as possible, so they can make it easier to learn their real versions.

3. Play actual songs: Let players practice their favourite songs inside the game, trying to play them at full speed with no mistakes.
How it started:
I was playing around with different VR prototypes, wanting to create a game that could only work with and made full use of VR’s unique input possibilities. Around the same time, I was debating buying a bass guitar, but decided not to because I didn’t know if I’d enjoy playing it or get bored shortly after.

However, that led me to think about how (and if) an actual guitar could be made in a VR game. I wanted to recreate playing a physical guitar, where you would need to actually strum and fret each string to play the song, rather than a simplified, arcade-style approach such as Guitar Hero.

I made a very crude M.V.P. the next day where you would ‘fret’ by holding the trigger button to press on all the strings at once and ‘pluck’ strings by pressing the face buttons.

Despite its simplicity, practising 'Seven Nation Army' on it got me immediately hooked.  As I kept developing the game, I would debug something and end up spending another 20 minutes just practising the song to play it better.
The game didn't start with a clear direction of what I wanted the final product to be. Instead, I started from the prototype and I analysed what made it feel so fun to play and expand on those features.

This was a challenge at times, as it often meant building and then throwing away many concepts that didn't add enough to the core experience.

At first the game was meant to be a rhythm game, but this felt at odds with the concept of playing real instruments since they would've needed to be simplified to make the VR inputs precise enough.

Because of this, the design shifted from the typical gameplay challenge of hitting all the right notes at the right time to a more relaxed "play at your own pace and learn how the instruments work" experience. Designing Instruments:
Following from the design pillars of the game, my priorities when making the instruments have always been:

#1 recreate the playing experience of the real instrument.
#2 make them simple and intuitive to play.
#3 allow the player to play the vast majority of songs.

Having the instruments resemble their real-life versions as much as possible is vital to make sure the players are actually getting a real feel for how playing those instruments is like. If the instrument is too far removed from the real thing, then learning to play them in game is more or less pointless.

Unfortunately, hardware limitations make a 1:1 copy impossible to achieve, so some adjustments need to be made. For instance, when playing the violin, the musician places their fingers at very specific positions on the fretboard, however, finger tracking technology is currently unable to track these movements reliably, so instead a radial menu is used to allow fast adjustments instead.
When moving from the MVP, the first instrument I tried to implement was the violin. I wanted to choose the most complex instrument I could to see what the limits of what can be done in the game are, so if I had to pivot from the design direction, I could do so as early as possible.

* updated the fretting system so it is continuous and notes are spaced correctly
* make the violin follow the shoulder's position and points to the hand
* bowing the strings when the bow is on the strings
Similarly, I thought making a guitar would also be impossible, as it would need to fret and strum multiple strings as the same time, which would not be possible to do with a standard VR controller.

Even a controller like the one on the Valve Index, which can detect individual finger placement couldn't also track their positions, so you still could not fret different strings at different positions.

* radial menu to select chord and strum patterns
* still not ideal since it doesn't feel like playing on a real guitar and very complex for a first time player
* to mitigate: kept the simple fretting and strumming systems and only have to use the menus when playing chords
